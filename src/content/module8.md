## Lesson 8.1 — OSHA Overview

The **Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA)** is the federal agency within the U.S. Department of Labor responsible for establishing and enforcing workplace safety standards.

### Key OSHA Standards for Plumbers

| Standard | Title | Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| 29 CFR 1926 | Construction Industry Safety Standards | Applies to all new construction plumbing |
| 29 CFR 1910 | General Industry Safety Standards | Applies to maintenance/service plumbing in existing buildings |
| 1926.502 | Fall Protection | Working at height on ladders, scaffolds, elevated slabs |
| 1926.651–652 | Excavations | Trenching for underground plumbing |
| 1926.21 | Safety Training | Employer must train workers on job hazards |
| 1910.146 | Permit-Required Confined Spaces | Manholes, vaults, tanks |
| 1910.1000 | Air Contaminants (PELs) | Soldering fumes, solvent vapors |

### The General Duty Clause (Section 5(a)(1))

Every employer must provide a workplace "free from recognized hazards that are causing or are likely to cause death or serious physical harm." This catch-all applies even when no specific OSHA standard covers a particular hazard.

### OSHA Penalties (2024 levels)

| Violation Type | Maximum Penalty per Violation |
|---|---|
| Other-than-serious | $16,131 |
| Serious | $16,131 |
| Willful or Repeated | $161,323 |

Penalties accumulate per instance per day the violation continues.

### Minimum Baseline PPE (all plumbing work)

| PPE Item | Hazard Protected Against |
|---|---|
| Safety glasses (ANSI Z87.1) | Flying debris, chips, splash |
| Work gloves | Cuts, abrasion, burns |
| Steel-toed work boots | Falling objects, puncture |
| Hard hat (ANSI Z89.1) | Overhead falling objects (required on most construction sites) |

### Task-Specific PPE

| Task | Additional PPE Required |
|---|---|
| Torch / soldering work | Face shield (ANSI Z87.1), leather gloves, fire extinguisher nearby |
| Grinding / cutting | Face shield, hearing protection (> 85 dBA for 8 hrs) |
| Drain cleaning (machine) | Face shield, chemical-resistant gloves (sewer exposure) |
| Working with solvents (PVC cement, primer) | Nitrile gloves, adequate ventilation; respirator (half-face with organic vapor cartridge) in confined spaces |
| Overhead work | Hard hat, safety glasses |
| Working at height (> 6 ft in construction) | Fall protection: harness and lanyard, or guardrails, or safety net |
| Trenching / excavation | Hard hat; cave-in protection system (see Lesson 8.4) |

## Lesson 8.3 — Confined Space Entry

### Definition

A **confined space** is any space that:
1. Is large enough for a worker to enter and perform work
2. Has limited or restricted means of entry or exit
3. Is not designed for continuous occupancy

**Examples in plumbing:** Manholes, wet wells, valve vaults, crawl spaces, large water storage tanks, underground pump stations.

### Permit-Required vs. Non-Permit Confined Space

| Type | Characteristics | Entry Procedure |
|---|---|---|
| **Non-permit confined space** | Contains no serious safety or health hazards | Entry with standard precautions (ventilation, communication) |
| **Permit-required confined space (PRCS)** | Contains or has potential for hazardous atmosphere, engulfment, entrapment, or other serious hazard | Full permit program required |

**Diagram placeholder:** `[FIGURE 8-2: Confined space hazard identification flowchart — 3-D decision tree]`

### Permit-Required Confined Space Entry Program (1910.146)

A full PRCS entry requires:

1. **Written permit** signed by the entry supervisor listing: location, purpose, duration, hazards, controls, attendant names, entrant names, emergency procedures.
2. **Atmospheric testing** (with a calibrated, multi-gas meter) before entry:
   - Oxygen: Must be 19.5 %–23.5 %
   - Flammable gases: Must be < 10 % of Lower Explosive Limit (LEL)
   - Toxic gases (H₂S): Must be < OSHA PEL (20 ppm for H₂S)
3. **Continuous forced-air ventilation** during entry (never oxygen enrichment).
4. **Attendant (hole watch)** stationed outside the space at all times; never enters.
5. **Non-entry rescue equipment** (tripod and winch, lifeline) required when entrant cannot self-rescue.
6. **Emergency rescue plan** communicated to entrants and local emergency services if needed.

> ⚠ More construction workers die each year in confined space accidents trying to rescue incapacitated coworkers than in the original incident. **Never enter a confined space to rescue someone unless you are trained and equipped.** Activate emergency services.

## Lesson 8.4 — Excavation and Trenching Safety

Underground plumbing often requires trenching for service lines, building sewers, and water service.

### OSHA 1926.651–652 Requirements

**Competent Person:** Every excavation must be directed by a designated competent person who can identify existing and predictable hazards and has the authority to take corrective action.

**Cave-in protection:** Required for all excavations 5 feet or deeper (or shallower if conditions are unstable). Three systems are permitted:

| System | Description |
|---|---|
| **Sloping** | Cut the trench walls at a safe angle (depends on soil type — Type A: 3/4:1; Type B: 1:1; Type C: 1.5:1) |
| **Shoring** | Install structural supports (timber, aluminum hydraulic shores) against the trench walls |
| **Trench box (shield)** | Pre-fabricated steel box dragged along the trench; workers work inside the box |

**Access and egress:** Ladders must be within 25 feet of workers in trenches 4 feet or deeper.

**Surface water and rain:** Inspect trench after every rain event; pump standing water before entering.

## Lesson 8.5 — Lead Safety (EPA RRP Rule)

### The Hazard

Lead was used in plumbing solder (up to 50 % lead) until the Safe Drinking Water Act of 1986, and in pipe materials until the early twentieth century. It is also found in lead-based paint throughout pre-1978 buildings.

When renovating, repairing, or painting (RRP) pre-1978 buildings with children under 6 or pregnant women present, federal EPA regulations (40 CFR Part 745) require:

### EPA RRP Firm Certification

Contractors performing RRP activities in pre-1978 child-occupied facilities must be **EPA-certified RRP firms** and use **EPA-certified renovators**.

### Lead-Safe Work Practices

1. **Contain the work area** with plastic sheeting; seal HVAC vents.
2. **Avoid practices that create lead dust:** No dry sanding, no heat guns > 1100 °F, no power tools without HEPA vacuum attachments.
3. **Wear PPE:** N100 or P100 respirator, disposable coveralls, gloves, shoe covers.
4. **Clean up:** Wet-mop and HEPA vacuum all surfaces; dispose of waste as lead debris.
5. **Post-renovation cleaning verification** and occupant notification.

> ⚠ Replacing lead supply pipes (**lead service line replacement**) is a major public-health initiative. Many utilities are funding replacements under EPA's Lead and Copper Rule (LCR) revisions. This is a growing specialty for plumbers.

## Lesson 8.6 — Jobsite Organization and Professionalism

### The 5S Methodology on a Plumbing Job Site

| S | Japanese | Meaning | Plumbing Application |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sort** | Seiri | Remove unnecessary items | Remove unused materials from the work area daily |
| **Set in order** | Seiton | Organize remaining items | Tool crib, pipe racks, labeled bins |
| **Shine** | Seiso | Clean the work area | Clean up filings, solder scraps, dirt daily |
| **Standardize** | Seiketsu | Establish standards | Consistent tool bag layout; labeling conventions |
| **Sustain** | Shitsuke | Maintain the discipline | Foreman audits; daily walkthrough |

### Customer Communication

Professional plumbers understand that technical skill alone doesn't build a business — communication does.

**Best practices:**
- **Call ahead** before arriving at a residential job; confirm appointment.
- **Wear shoe covers** when entering a finished home.
- **Explain the work** before starting in clear, non-technical terms.
- **Provide written estimates** that itemize labor and materials.
- **Document any pre-existing damage** (photos) before beginning work.
- **Explain what you found** at the end of the job, not just the invoice.

## Lesson 8.7 — Basic Estimating

### Components of a Plumbing Estimate

| Component | Description |
|---|---|
| **Material takeoff** | Counting every pipe, fitting, valve, and fixture from the plans; applying current pricing |
| **Labor hours** | Estimating time per task × crew wage rate (including burden: taxes, insurance, benefits) |
| **Equipment** | Rental or owned tool costs (threading machine, press tool, torch) |
| **Subcontractors** | Specialty items (testing, specialized equipment) |
| **Overhead** | Company operating costs allocated per job (office, vehicles, insurance) |
| **Profit margin** | Net profit desired (typically 10–20 % for plumbing contractors) |

**Bid price = Material + Labor + Equipment + Subcontractors + Overhead + Profit**

### Unit-Price Estimating

Many estimators use **unit prices** — a pre-calculated cost per fixture or per linear foot of pipe that bundles material, labor, and overhead into a single number. Unit prices must be updated regularly to reflect current material costs and wage rates.

**Example:**
- Rough-in and set a toilet = $350 labor + $85 fixture + $30 materials = $465 unit price
- Install 1/2" copper supply pipe = $8.50 per linear foot installed (material + labor)

## Module 8 Summary

- OSHA 29 CFR 1926 governs construction plumbing; the General Duty Clause covers all recognized hazards
- Baseline PPE: safety glasses, gloves, steel-toed boots, hard hat; add task-specific PPE as required
- Confined space entry requires atmospheric testing, ventilation, attendant, written permit, and rescue plan
- Trenching ≥ 5 feet requires cave-in protection: sloping, shoring, or a trench box
- EPA RRP Rule requires certified firm/renovator for work in pre-1978 buildings with children or pregnant women
- Professional plumbers combine technical skill with organized job sites, clear communication, and accurate estimating
