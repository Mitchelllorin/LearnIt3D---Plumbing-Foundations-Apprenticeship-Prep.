## Lesson 3.2 — Copper Pipe

Copper has been the dominant water-supply material in North America for more than 70 years because of its proven longevity (50+ year service life), bacteriostatic properties, and ability to withstand pressure cycling and temperature extremes.

### Copper Tube Types

| Type | Wall Thickness | Color Code | Common Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Type K** | Thickest | Green | Underground, high-pressure |
| **Type L** | Medium | Blue | Residential/commercial supply (most common) |
| **Type M** | Thinnest | Red | Residential supply (check local code) |
| **Type DWV** | Special | Yellow | Drain, waste, vent (rare today) |

**Note:** Type designations refer to wall thickness, not outside diameter. For a given nominal size, all types share the **same outside diameter** (OD), which is always 1/8" larger than the nominal size (e.g., 1/2" nominal copper = 5/8" OD).

### Copper Supply Forms
- **Hard-drawn (straight lengths):** 20-foot sticks; used for all exposed and in-wall supply runs.
- **Soft (annealed) coils:** 50- and 100-foot coils; used for underground service lines, refrigerator supply, ice-maker lines, and wherever bending is needed.

### Joining Copper
1. **Sweat soldering (brazing):** The most common method. Lead-free solder (minimum 95 % tin) is required for potable water. Use water-soluble flux; remove flux residue after soldering.
2. **Press fittings:** Cold-joining; no torch. Requires press tool with manufacturer-approved jaws.
3. **Flare fittings:** Used on soft copper for gas service, refrigeration, and some compressed-air applications. Requires a flaring tool.
4. **Compression fittings:** Used for supply-stop connections and finish-plumbing connections. No heat required.

## Lesson 3.3 — PEX (Cross-Linked Polyethylene)

PEX is now the most-installed water-supply pipe material in new residential construction in North America. Its flexibility, freeze-damage resistance, and easy installation drove rapid market adoption after code acceptance in the 1990s–2000s.

### PEX Grades

| Grade | Cross-Linking Method | Flexibility | Memory | Common Brand |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **PEX-A** | Peroxide (Engel method) | Most flexible | Highest (expansion fittings) | Uponor (Wirsbo) |
| **PEX-B** | Silane (moisture cure) | Medium | Medium (crimp/clamp) | Viega, generic |
| **PEX-C** | Electron beam | Least flexible | Lowest | Rare |

**Key advantages of PEX:**
- Flexible — can be bent around corners with a bend support, reducing fittings
- Freeze-tolerant — expands and contracts without cracking (especially PEX-A)
- Quiet — no thermal expansion "ticking" as dramatic as copper
- Corrosion-resistant — does not corrode in aggressive water (acidic pH, high chloramine)

**Key limitations:**
- Cannot be used above 200 °F
- Must be protected from UV light (not for exposed outdoor runs)
- Not approved for gas in most jurisdictions
- Some localities still restrict PEX in commercial applications

### PEX Color Coding Convention
- **Red** = Hot water supply
- **Blue** = Cold water supply
- **White / gray** = Either (check installer markings)

## Lesson 3.4 — CPVC (Chlorinated Polyvinyl Chloride)

CPVC is a rigid plastic pipe approved for both hot and cold potable water. It was the dominant plastic supply material before PEX became widely code-accepted.

- **Standard:** ASTM D2846 (CTS — Copper Tube Size)
- **Temperature rating:** 200 °F / 100 psi
- **Joining:** Solvent cement — apply primer, then CPVC-specific orange or gray cement

**Important:** CPVC uses **CTS (Copper Tube Size)** dimensions — its OD matches copper of the same nominal size. Do not confuse with PVC, which uses IPS (Iron Pipe Size) OD dimensions. They are not interchangeable, even though fittings look similar.

## Lesson 3.5 — PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride)

PVC Schedule 40 is the workhorse of residential DWV systems, cold water supply mains, and underground irrigation. It is not approved for hot water supply.

### PVC Schedules

| Schedule | Wall Thickness | Pressure Rating (1/2") | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Schedule 40** | Standard | 600 psi | DWV, irrigation, cold supply |
| **Schedule 80** | Heavier | 850 psi | Industrial, higher pressure |
| **Class 200** | Thin | 200 psi | Underground irrigation only |

### Solvent Cementing PVC
1. **Cut square** with a PVC cutter, miter box, or saw.
2. **Deburr and chamfer** the pipe end with a chamfering tool or utility knife (reduce by about 10–15° to avoid wiping cement off during insertion).
3. **Apply primer** (purple-dyed for inspection visibility) to the pipe OD and fitting socket ID.
4. **Apply PVC cement** to both surfaces immediately after priming; insert pipe with a 1/4 turn and hold for 30 seconds.
5. Allow full cure per manufacturer's recommendations before pressure testing (typically 2 hours at 70 °F; longer in cold weather).

> ⚠ **Solvent cement is not a glue.** It chemically fuses the plastic surfaces — the bond is actually stronger than the pipe itself when fully cured.

## Lesson 3.6 — ABS (Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene)

ABS is a black, rigid plastic pipe used exclusively for **DWV** (drain, waste, and vent) applications in residential construction. It is the dominant DWV material in California and many western states; PVC DWV is more common in the east.

- **Standard:** ASTM D2661
- **Joining:** One-step ABS solvent cement (no primer required by code, but primer improves bond reliability in cold weather)
- **Cannot be mixed with PVC** using standard cement; use a **transition cement** where the two materials must be joined

**ABS vs. PVC DWV — At a Glance:**
| Property | ABS | PVC |
|---|---|---|
| Color | Black | White or cream |
| Weight | Lighter | Heavier |
| Thermal expansion | More | Less |
| UV resistance | Better | Poorer |
| Flame spread | Higher | Lower |

## Lesson 3.7 — Cast Iron Soil Pipe

Cast iron (CI) is the traditional DWV material for multi-story and commercial buildings. It remains the premium choice where noise isolation is critical (hotels, hospitals, multi-family).

### CI Joining Systems

**Hub-and-spigot (lead and oakum — legacy):**
The spigot end of one pipe fits into the hub (bell) of the next. Traditionally sealed with oakum (hemp) and molten lead — now replaced with compression gaskets for all new work.

**No-hub (hubless):**
Adopted in the 1960s. Pipe ends butt together; a **no-hub coupling** (rubber gasket + stainless band clamp) seals the joint. The standard no-hub coupling is CISPI 310; heavy-duty (shielded) couplings are required by some codes for underground and concealed locations.

**Diagram placeholder:** `[FIGURE 3-3: No-hub cast iron coupling — exploded 3-D view]`

### Cast Iron Cutting Methods
- **Snap cutter (chain cutter):** Wraps around the pipe; tightened chain scores the pipe and breaks it with a sharp snap — fast and clean.
- **Angle grinder with cut-off wheel:** Used where a snap cutter can't fit.
- **Reciprocating saw with bi-metal blade:** Slow but works in any orientation.

## Lesson 3.8 — Fittings

Fittings change direction, transition between sizes, and connect branches. Understanding fitting geometry is critical to accurate layout and code compliance.

### Common Fitting Types

**Diagram placeholder:** `[FIGURE 3-4: Fitting identification wall chart — 3-D interactive, click any fitting for name and use]`

| Fitting | Description | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **90° Elbow** | 90° direction change | Supply and DWV |
| **45° Elbow** | 45° direction change | Offset routing |
| **Coupling** | Straight connection | Extend pipe length |
| **Tee** | Three-way branch | Branch lines |
| **Wye (Y)** | 45° branch | DWV preferred over tees for drain lines |
| **Combo (Long-Sweep Y + 1/8 bend)** | DWV only | Closet bends, standard drain branch |
| **Reducer / Reducer Bushing** | Transition two sizes | Size changes |
| **Union** | Inline disconnect | Service point |
| **Dielectric Union** | Dissimilar metal isolation | Copper-to-galvanized transition |
| **Cap** | Close pipe end | Pressure testing, cleanout plugs |
| **Plug** | Close fitting socket | Unused ports |
| **Cleanout (CO)** | Access for drain cleaning | Required at base of stack, direction changes > 135° |
| **P-Trap** | Creates water seal | All fixture drain connections |
| **Sanitary Tee** | DWV vertical stack | Branch to stack (vertical to horizontal prohibited) |
| **Long-Turn 90° (Sweep 90°)** | DWV horizontal run | Allows drain snake passage |

### DWV Fitting Rules
- **Never use a standard (short-pattern) tee on horizontal drain runs** — it creates a 90° entry that traps solids.
- Use a **wye-and-eighth bend** or **combo fitting** to connect branch drains to horizontal runs.
- A **sanitary tee** is only approved for connecting a horizontal branch to a vertical stack (or for vent piping).
- All DWV direction changes greater than 45° on horizontal runs require **long-sweep fittings** to maintain drain flow and allow rodding.

## Lesson 3.9 — Dissimilar Metals and Galvanic Corrosion

When two dissimilar metals are in contact in the presence of an electrolyte (water), an electrochemical reaction occurs called **galvanic corrosion**. The less noble metal (anode) corrodes and sacrifices itself to protect the more noble metal (cathode).

**Galvanic Series (Partial — most noble at top):**
```
Gold (most noble / cathodic — protected)
Copper / Bronze / Brass
Tin
Lead
Cast Iron
Carbon Steel / Mild Steel
Zinc (galvanized coating)
Magnesium (least noble / anodic — most at risk)
```

**Common plumbing scenario:** Connecting copper supply pipe directly to a galvanized steel nipple will cause the galvanized steel to corrode rapidly because copper is more noble.

**Solution:** Install a **dielectric union** or **dielectric nipple** between the two materials. The dielectric union has a plastic sleeve and washer that electrically isolate the two metals, preventing current flow and stopping galvanic action.

## Module 3 Summary

- Common pipe materials: **copper, PEX, CPVC, PVC, ABS, cast iron, galvanized, black iron**
- Each material has specific approved uses, temperature limits, and required joining methods
- DWV fittings must be long-sweep to maintain flow; sanitary tees are limited to vertical stacks
- Dissimilar metals must be isolated with dielectric unions to prevent galvanic corrosion
