## Lesson 5.1 — Principles of Gravity Drainage

Unlike the water supply system, which uses pressure to push water uphill, the **DWV system relies entirely on gravity**. Waste flows downhill by virtue of slope; the plumber's job is to maintain the correct slope, avoid obstructions, and provide adequate venting so air can replace the draining water column.

### Flow in Drain Pipes: The Self-Cleansing Velocity

Drain pipes must flow at a velocity sufficient to carry solids in suspension — this is called the **self-cleansing velocity**, typically defined as **2 feet per second (0.6 m/s)** minimum.

The slope required to achieve self-cleansing velocity depends on pipe size. Flatter slopes let solids settle; steeper slopes let water rush past the solids and leave them behind.

**Code-required slopes (IPC 704.1 / UPC 708.0):**

| Pipe Size | Minimum Slope | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 2-1/2" and smaller | 1/4" per foot (2 %) | Standard residential |
| 3" and 4" | 1/4" per foot (2 %) preferred | 1/8" per foot acceptable |
| 5" and larger | 1/8" per foot (1 %) | Building drain / sewer |

> Maximum slope is also limited — pipe sloped greater than 45° is considered vertical and must follow stack rules.

## Lesson 5.2 — DWV System Components

A complete DWV system consists of the following interconnected elements:

```
FIXTURE (toilet, sink, tub)
    │  fixture drain
    ▼
TRAP (water seal — blocks sewer gas)
    │  trap arm
    ▼
BRANCH DRAIN (horizontal collecting pipe)
    │
    ▼
SOIL or WASTE STACK (vertical pipe; soil = carries toilet waste)
    │
    ▼
BUILDING DRAIN (horizontal, lowest drain in the building)
    │
    ▼
BUILDING SEWER (outside the foundation → to public sewer or septic)
    │
    ▼
PUBLIC SEWER / SEPTIC SYSTEM
```

**Diagram placeholder:** `[FIGURE 5-2: Full-building DWV isometric — 3-D interactive, click any component to highlight and annotate]`

### Soil Pipe vs. Waste Pipe
- **Soil pipe** conveys the discharge of one or more water closets (toilets), with or without other fixtures.
- **Waste pipe** conveys the discharge of fixtures other than water closets.

Both ultimately connect to the building drain and sewer.

### Stack vs. Branch
- **Stack:** Vertical DWV pipe that extends one or more floors.
- **Branch drain:** Horizontal pipe that collects from one or more fixtures and connects to a stack or building drain.

## Lesson 5.3 — Traps

A **trap** is a fitting designed to hold a water seal that prevents sewer gas — a mixture of methane, hydrogen sulfide, ammonia, and other gases — from entering the building. Every fixture must be individually trapped (unless a code-approved combination fixture trap is used).

**Diagram placeholder:** `[FIGURE 5-3: P-trap cross-section — 3-D cutaway showing water seal depth]`

### The P-Trap

The **P-trap** is the universal fixture trap in modern plumbing. It consists of a curved section that retains a **2-inch deep water seal** (code minimum per IPC 1002.1 and UPC 1002.1; max 4 inches for standard traps).

P-trap components:
- **Inlet (from fixture):** Receives fixture discharge
- **Crown weir:** The top of the trap curve — sets the water level
- **Water seal:** The depth of water retained in the trap between the crown weir and the dip
- **Trap arm:** The outlet section connecting the trap to the drain branch or stack

### Prohibited Traps
The following trap types are prohibited because they cannot maintain a reliable seal (IPC 1003):
- **S-trap:** The outlet curves downward immediately after the crown weir, creating siphon conditions
- **Bell trap / drum trap:** Seals dependent on debris — unreliable
- **Crown-vented trap:** Vent connected at the crown weir — siphons the seal
- **Running trap:** Obsolete; prohibited except where required to re-trap a fixture already connected to a trapped branch

### Causes of Trap Seal Loss

| Cause | Mechanism | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| **Self-siphonage** | Fixture discharge creates siphon in its own trap | Proper vent within required distance |
| **Induced siphonage** | Adjacent fixture discharge creates negative pressure in branch, siphons nearby traps | Common/circuit venting, adequate branch size |
| **Back-pressure** | Positive pressure in the drain pushes water out of the trap into the room | Proper vent and stack sizing |
| **Evaporation** | Water seal evaporates in infrequently used fixtures | Pour water into floor drains periodically; install trap primers |

## Lesson 5.4 — Venting

Venting is the system of pipes that admits atmospheric air to the DWV system. Without venting:
1. The drain system cannot flow freely — a partial vacuum forms behind each slug of draining water, slowing flow.
2. Trap seals are siphoned out by negative pressure.
3. Positive pressure from stack flow can blow sewer gas through trap seals.

### Vent Pipe Functions
1. **Equalize pressure** throughout the DWV system
2. **Protect trap seals** by admitting air to prevent siphonage and back-pressure
3. **Discharge toxic and flammable gases** safely to the atmosphere above the roof

### Individual Vent (Back Vent)
The simplest and most reliable vent type: a vent pipe connected to the trap arm of a single fixture and extended to open air above the roof (or to a vent stack).

**Diagram placeholder:** `[FIGURE 5-4: Individual vent diagram — 3-D building cross-section showing fixture, trap arm, and individual vent rising to roof]`

### Distance from Trap to Vent (Trap Arm Length)
The trap arm must connect to the vent before the maximum allowed distance is exceeded (IPC Table 909.1 / UPC Table 10-1):

| Trap Arm Pipe Size | Maximum Trap Arm Length |
|---|---|
| 1-1/4" | 2'–6" |
| 1-1/2" | 3'–6" |
| 2" | 5' |
| 3" | 6' |
| 4" | 10' |

### Common Vent
Two fixtures on the same floor, back-to-back, can share a single vent (common vent). Both traps must be at the same level, and the vent must connect at or above the flood-level rim of the higher fixture.

### Wet Vent
A **wet vent** serves as both a drain for an upstream fixture AND a vent for a downstream fixture. This saves pipe and fittings in bathroom groups. Code limits on size and fixture count apply (IPC 908 / UPC 908).

**Example:** The 2" drain from a lavatory wet-vents the tub below it in the same bathroom group.

### Stack Vent vs. Vent Stack
- **Stack vent:** The extension of the soil or waste stack above the highest fixture connection — goes straight up through the roof.
- **Vent stack:** A separate vertical vent pipe that runs parallel to the soil/waste stack and connects at the base (to equalize pressure) and at the top (terminates above the roof or connects to the stack vent).

Buildings with multiple bathroom groups on multiple floors require a vent stack to prevent pressure build-up.

### Circuit Vent
Used in commercial buildings — a single vent pipe serves a horizontal branch with multiple fixtures. Permitted for 8 or fewer fixtures on a horizontal branch (IPC 911). Requires a relief vent at the upstream end.

### Air Admittance Valve (AAV)

**Diagram placeholder:** `[FIGURE 5-5: AAV cross-section — 3-D showing negative-pressure opening and gravity-close operation]`

An AAV is a mechanical one-way valve that opens under negative pressure (allows air in) and closes under positive pressure or gravity (seals against sewer gas). They are used to eliminate the need for individual through-roof vents.

**AAV applications:**
- Island sinks (where a through-roof vent is not feasible)
- Additions and remodels where running a vent to the roof is impractical
- Some jurisdictions prohibit AAVs in certain locations (check local adoption)

**AAV requirements:**
- Listed to ASSE 1051 (individual fixture) or ASSE 1050 (branch/stack — Studor Maxi-Vent type)
- Must be accessible for inspection and replacement
- Cannot be the sole vent for a building — at least one stack vent must penetrate the roof to relieve positive pressure

## Lesson 5.5 — Cleanouts

**Cleanouts** provide access to drain lines for maintenance and clearing blockages.

**Code-required cleanout locations (IPC 708 / UPC 707):**
- At the base of every soil and waste stack
- At every change of horizontal direction greater than 135° (45° total)
- At intervals not exceeding 100 feet in horizontal drain runs
- At the building drain connection near the foundation wall (exterior cleanout or building sewer cleanout)
- At the junction of the building drain and building sewer

**Cleanout access:** Cleanouts must be accessible — either exposed or behind an access panel. They may not be concealed in finished walls without a listed access cover.

**Cleanout sizing:** Cleanout fittings must be at least half the size of the pipe they serve up to 4"; 4" cleanout for 4" and larger pipe.

## Lesson 5.6 — Interceptors and Separators

Certain commercial establishments must pre-treat their wastewater before it enters the public sewer system:

| Device | Purpose | Required For |
|---|---|---|
| **Grease interceptor (trap)** | Separates fats, oils, and grease (FOG) | Restaurants, institutional kitchens |
| **Sand/sediment interceptor** | Settles heavy solids | Car washes, laundries, floor drains in garages |
| **Oil separator** | Removes petroleum hydrocarbons | Parking garages, gas stations |
| **Lint interceptor** | Captures laundry lint | Commercial laundries |

Grease interceptors must be sized by flow rate and grease-retention capacity (PDI G101 standard). Most jurisdictions require a maintenance log and regular pumping schedule.

## Module 5 Summary

- DWV systems rely on gravity; the correct slope (¼" per foot for ≤ 3" pipe) ensures self-cleansing velocity
- Every fixture must be trapped; P-traps with a 2"–4" water seal are the standard
- Trap seal loss can be caused by self-siphonage, induced siphonage, back-pressure, or evaporation
- Venting protects trap seals and allows free drain flow; types include individual, common, wet, circuit, and AAV
- Cleanouts are required at stack bases, direction changes > 135°, and every 100 feet
- Commercial establishments require interceptors for grease, sand, and oil
