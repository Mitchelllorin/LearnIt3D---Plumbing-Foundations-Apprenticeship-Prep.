## Lesson 2.1 — Tool Safety Fundamentals

Before handling any tool, review the four cardinal rules of trade-tool safety:

1. **Inspect before use.** Check blades for cracks, handles for splinters or cracks, and cords/hoses for fraying before every use.
2. **Use the right tool for the job.** Improvising with the wrong tool damages both the tool and the work — and risks injury.
3. **Wear appropriate PPE.** At minimum: safety glasses, work gloves, and steel-toed boots. Additional PPE (face shield, hearing protection, respirator) when required by the task.
4. **Store tools properly.** Return tools to their cases or wall-mount positions after every use. A cluttered tool bag is a safety hazard and shortens tool life.

## Lesson 2.2 — Measuring & Layout Tools

### Tape Measure
A **25-foot locking tape measure** is the most-used tool in a plumber's bag. Plumbers read tape measures in fractions of an inch (1/8, 1/16) and in decimal equivalents for pipe calculations.

**Key skill:** Measure to the center of a fitting rather than to its face when calculating rough-in dimensions (center-to-center vs. face-to-face measurement).

### Level
- **4-foot torpedo level** — used to set horizontal drain lines to the required slope (¼" per foot for 3" and smaller drains; 1/8" per foot for 4" and larger — per IPC Table 704.1).
- **Digital level** — displays slope as a percentage or degree; useful for setting drains in tight spaces.

### Chalk Line
Used to snap long, straight layout lines on floors, walls, or ceilings for multi-fixture drain runs and water-supply rough-ins.

### Pipe Marking Gauge
A semicircular card or digital tool that helps a pipefitter mark the exact depth a pipe must be inserted into a fitting to achieve the correct engagement length.

## Lesson 2.3 — Cutting Tools

### Pipe Cutter (Wheel Cutter)

**Diagram placeholder:** `[FIGURE 2-1: Pipe cutter — 3-D interactive model, click to animate rotation]`

A pipe cutter produces a clean, square cut on copper, CPVC, and thin-wall stainless tubing. The hardened steel cutting wheel scores the pipe; tightening the feed screw and rotating the tool deepens the score until the pipe severs.

**Sizes:**
- Mini cutters: 1/8"–1" — for tight spaces
- Standard cutters: 1/4"–2-1/2"
- Large cutters: up to 4"

**Operating procedure:**
1. Mark the cut line.
2. Clamp the cutter onto the pipe with the wheel on the mark; snug the feed screw.
3. Rotate the cutter 360° around the pipe.
4. Advance the feed screw 1/4 turn after each full revolution.
5. Continue until the pipe separates.
6. **Deburr the cut end** before fitting assembly.

### Hacksaw
A hacksaw with a **32-TPI (teeth per inch)** blade cuts galvanized iron, stainless steel, and ABS/PVC pipe. For PVC and ABS, use 18–24 TPI. Keep the blade perpendicular to the pipe to ensure a square cut.

### Reciprocating Saw (Sawzall)
Used for rough cutting of cast iron, galvanized, and plastic drain pipe. Use a **bi-metal blade** for ferrous pipe and a **wood/demo blade** for plastic. Reciprocating saws remove pipe quickly during demo work but require careful placement to avoid cutting adjacent framing or wiring.

### Angle Grinder with Cut-Off Wheel
Used for cutting cast iron soil pipe and large-diameter stainless. Always use proper cut-off wheels rated for the material and RPM of the grinder. Wear a face shield — **not just safety glasses** — when cutting with a grinder.

### PVC/CPVC Ratchet Cutter
A scissor-action ratchet cutter produces fast, burr-free cuts on plastic pipe up to 1-1/4" without needing a vise. Squeeze and release the handles in a ratcheting motion to advance the blade.

## Lesson 2.4 — Pipe Preparation Tools

### Deburring Tool (Reamer)

**Diagram placeholder:** `[FIGURE 2-2: Deburring tool — 3-D interactive, click to see reaming action]`

After cutting copper tubing with a wheel cutter, a ridge (burr) forms on the inside diameter. This burr:
- Restricts flow
- Accelerates erosion at the cut end
- Creates turbulence that promotes erosion-corrosion in copper

The **deburring/reaming blade** on the back of many pipe cutters, or a standalone reamer, removes this burr by rotating the blade inside the tube end.

**Procedure:**
1. Insert the reaming blade into the cut end of the tube.
2. Rotate clockwise with moderate pressure for 3–5 full revolutions.
3. Blow out or wipe out metal filings before soldering.

### Emery Cloth / Sand Paper
After deburring, clean the outside of the copper tubing with **120-grit emery cloth** or a wire-brush fitting brush. Cleaning removes oxides and exposes fresh copper — essential for a sound soldered joint. Always clean both the tube OD and the fitting socket ID.

### Fitting Brush
A round wire brush sized to the fitting socket (1/2", 3/4", 1", etc.) cleans the inside of copper sweat fittings. Available in kit sets covering 1/2"–2" sizes.

## Lesson 2.5 — Joining Tools

### Propane / MAP-Pro Torch

**Diagram placeholder:** `[FIGURE 2-3: Propane torch — 3-D interactive, click to ignite flame]`

Used for **soldering (sweating)** copper water supply pipe and fittings using lead-free solder (required by law since the Safe Drinking Water Act of 1986, reinforced in 2014).

**Key torch types:**
| Fuel | Max Tip Temperature | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Propane | ~3,600 °F | Residential copper, 1/2"–2" |
| MAP-Pro | ~5,300 °F | Larger diameter copper, faster heating |
| Oxy-acetylene | ~5,600 °F | Bronze brazing (gas lines), large copper |

**Safe soldering procedure:**
1. Prepare the joint (cut, deburr, clean, apply flux).
2. Use a **flame shield** (fiberglass backer board) to protect adjacent framing.
3. Apply heat to the fitting body — not the solder. The fitting must be hot enough to melt the solder.
4. Touch lead-free solder to the joint gap (not the flame). Capillary action draws solder into the joint.
5. Allow to cool undisturbed for at least 30 seconds; wipe excess flux with a damp cloth.
6. **Test** the joint under working pressure before closing walls.

> ⚠ **Fire hazard:** Always carry a fire extinguisher when torch work is performed near combustible materials. Check for smoldering materials for 30 minutes after completing torch work.

### PEX Crimp Tool

**Diagram placeholder:** `[FIGURE 2-4: PEX crimp tool — 3-D interactive, squeeze to show crimp action]`

PEX (cross-linked polyethylene) pipe uses one of three joining systems:

| System | Tool Required | Ring Material |
|---|---|---|
| **Crimp** (ASTM F1807) | PEX crimp tool + go/no-go gauge | Copper crimp ring |
| **Clamp / cinch** (ASTM F2098) | Clamp tool | Stainless clamp |
| **Expansion** (ASTM F1960) | Expansion tool | Engineered fitting |

**Crimp procedure (ASTM F1807):**
1. Cut PEX square with a ratchet cutter.
2. Slide copper crimp ring 1/4"–1/8" from the pipe end.
3. Insert PEX fitting fully until the pipe shoulder is visible.
4. Position the crimp ring 1/8"–1/4" from the end.
5. Place the crimp tool jaws perpendicular to the pipe; squeeze fully closed in one stroke.
6. Check with the **go/no-go gauge** — the go side must pass; the no-go side must not.

### Press Tool (ProPress / MegaPress)

**Diagram placeholder:** `[FIGURE 2-5: Press tool — 3-D interactive, click to press fitting]`

Pressing tools use hydraulic or electro-hydraulic force to deform a pre-installed fitting sleeve around the pipe. Systems include:
- **Copper press** (Viega ProPress, NIBCO, etc.) — clean copper, no soldering, no flame
- **Carbon steel press** (Viega MegaPress) — black iron and galvanized up to 2"
- **Stainless press** — sanitary, high-purity applications

**Press procedure:**
1. Cut and deburr the pipe; wipe clean.
2. Insert pipe fully into the fitting (check the insertion-depth mark).
3. Snap the appropriate jaw set onto the fitting sleeve.
4. Squeeze the trigger; the tool cycles automatically until the press is complete.
5. Verify the fitting has been pressed (look for the "M" or "press" indent on the sleeve).

> ⚠ **Never press an unprepared fitting.** An unpressed fitting will appear installed but will leak under pressure.

### Pipe Wrench

**Diagram placeholder:** `[FIGURE 2-6: Pipe wrench — 3-D interactive, drag to rotate jaw]`

The pipe wrench is the iconic plumbing tool, used to tighten and loosen **threaded (NPT) pipe and fittings**. Its serrated jaws grip harder as torque is applied.

**Sizes:**
- 10" — for 1/2"–3/4" pipe
- 14" — for 3/4"–1-1/2" pipe
- 18" — for 1-1/2"–2" pipe
- 24" and 36" — for 2"–4" pipe

**Rules of use:**
- Always use **two wrenches** (one backup, one turning) when assembling threaded pipe to avoid twisting already-installed pipe.
- Apply force toward the open jaw — this is the gripping direction.
- Never use a pipe wrench on soft copper, chrome-plated, or plastic pipe; use smooth-jaw adjustable pliers or strap wrenches instead.
- Keep jaw serrations clean and sharp; replace worn jaws.

### Adjustable Pliers (Channel-Lock / Groove-Joint Pliers)
Tongue-and-groove adjustable pliers are used on supply stops, angle stops, compression fittings, nuts, and chromed trap assemblies where a pipe wrench would damage the finish.

## Lesson 2.6 — Threading Tools

### Pipe Threading Machine (Power Threader)

For threaded black iron, galvanized, and stainless pipe, a **threading machine** (e.g., RIDGID 300, 535) cuts NPT (National Pipe Taper) threads quickly and accurately.

**Diagram placeholder:** `[FIGURE 2-7: Pipe threading machine with die head — labeled 3-D diagram]`

**Operating procedure:**
1. Chuck the pipe in the machine; support the far end on a pipe stand.
2. Select the correct die head for the pipe size.
3. Apply **cutting oil** generously to cool the dies and carry away chips.
4. Engage the die head; run the machine at its rated speed.
5. Cut threads until the pipe end is flush with the die head face (standard engagement).
6. Back off the die head; measure threads with a thread gauge.
7. Clean threads; apply **PTFE tape** or **pipe-joint compound (dope)** before assembly.

### Hand Threader (Ratchet Die Stock)
For short cuts and on-site repairs where a machine isn't available, a ratchet handle and die set cuts threads by hand. Back off 1/4 turn every full turn to break chips.

## Lesson 2.7 — Drain & Sewer Tools

### Drain Machine (Snake / Auger)
- **Hand drum auger** (25 ft, 5/16" cable) — for 1-1/4"–2" lavatory and kitchen drains
- **Power drum machine** (100 ft, 1/2" cable) — for 3"–4" drain lines, shower pans, floor drains
- **Water jetter** — high-pressure water scours grease and scale from 3"–6" sewer lines

### Toilet Auger (Closet Auger)
A 3-foot auger with a protective sleeve prevents porcelain scratching while clearing clogs at or past the toilet's trap-way.

### Test Plug & Ball Inflation Plug
Inflatable test plugs seal drain pipes for pressure or flood tests during rough-in inspection.

---

## Lesson 2.8 — Drilling & Boring Tools

### Hole Saw Kit
Bi-metal hole saws cut access holes through:
- Wood framing for pipe run-through (common sizes: 1-3/8", 1-1/2", 2-1/8", 3-5/8")
- Drywall and sub-floor panels

**Fire blocking reminder:** Any hole through a fire-rated assembly must be firestopped with an approved intumescent collar or caulk after pipe installation.

### Self-Feed Bit (Nail-Eater Bit)
Carbide-tipped self-feed bits drill rapidly through dimensional lumber and can cut through nails, making them ideal for retrofit work in existing walls.

### Right-Angle Drill
A right-angle (or close-quarters) drill fits in 3.5-inch stud bays to bore through plates and blocking where a standard drill cannot fit.
