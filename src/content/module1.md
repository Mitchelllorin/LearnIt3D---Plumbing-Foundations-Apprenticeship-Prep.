## Lesson 1.1 — A Brief History of Plumbing

The word *plumbing* derives from the Latin **plumbum**, meaning lead — the metal Roman engineers used to fabricate water-distribution pipes more than 2,000 years ago. The Romans built aqueducts stretching hundreds of miles, delivering fresh water by gravity to fountains, bathhouses, and private homes. While their engineering was impressive, the use of lead pipes contributed to chronic lead poisoning, a public-health hazard not fully understood until the nineteenth century.

### Ancient Civilizations

| Civilization | Contribution | Approximate Date |
|---|---|---|
| Indus Valley (Mohenjo-Daro) | Underground brick sewers with inspection ports | 2500 BCE |
| Minoan Crete | Terracotta drain pipes and flush latrines | 1700 BCE |
| Ancient Rome | Lead supply pipes, aqueducts, public baths | 300 BCE–400 CE |
| Medieval Europe | Rudimentary stone gutters; most waste dumped in streets | 500–1400 CE |

### The Sanitary Revolution (1840s–1900s)

The outbreak of cholera in London during the 1840s forced city authorities to link disease to contaminated water. Engineer Joseph Bazalgette designed London's famous intercepting sewer system (completed 1875), separating waste from drinking water and dramatically reducing mortality. In the United States, Boston installed its first sewer system in 1833; by 1900 most large American cities had municipal water and sewer services.

### The Modern Era

The twentieth century brought standardized pipe materials (iron, copper, later plastics), the development of the trap-and-vent principle, and the formalization of plumbing codes. Today, licensed plumbers install and maintain the systems that supply clean water, remove waste, and distribute gas and hydronic heating — making the trade one of the most important in construction and building maintenance.

## Lesson 1.2 — The Structure of the Plumbing Trade

### License Levels

#### Apprentice
An apprentice is enrolled in a Joint Apprenticeship and Training Committee (JATC) or union-affiliated program. Training typically spans **four to five years** and combines:
- On-the-job training (OJT) — a minimum of 2,000 hours per year under a journeyman or master
- Related Technical Instruction (RTI) — approximately 144 classroom/lab hours per year
- Progressive wage increases (commonly 50 % of journeyman scale in Year 1, rising to 90 % by Year 4–5)

#### Journeyman
After completing the apprenticeship and passing a state or local licensing exam, a plumber earns journeyman status. Journeymen can work independently on permitted projects but may not pull permits in most jurisdictions.

#### Master Plumber
A master plumber has typically worked as a journeyman for two to four years and has passed a more comprehensive exam covering code, design, and business law. Masters can:
- Apply for and hold plumbing permits
- Supervise apprentices and journeymen
- Own and operate a plumbing contracting business

### Specialty Certifications
Beyond the core license, plumbers may obtain specialized endorsements:
- **Backflow Preventer Tester** — required to test and certify cross-connection control devices
- **Medical Gas Installer** — NFPA 99 / ASSE 6010
- **Fire Suppression** — NICET certification levels I–IV
- **Green Plumber** — water-efficiency and sustainability practices

## Lesson 1.3 — Trade Unions and Professional Associations

### United Association (UA)
The **United Association of Journeymen and Apprentices of the Plumbing and Pipe Fitting Industry of the United States, Canada and Australia** (commonly called the UA) is the largest labor organization in the plumbing and piping trades. Founded in 1889, the UA:
- Operates JATCs in hundreds of locals across North America
- Sets curriculum standards for apprenticeship and continuing education
- Negotiates wages and benefits through collective-bargaining agreements
- Offers specialized training (Medical Gas, Welding, Service Technician, etc.)

### PHCC — Plumbing-Heating-Cooling Contractors Association
The PHCC represents independent plumbing and HVAC contractors. It provides:
- Business and financial training for contractor-owners
- Legislative advocacy at state and federal levels
- The PHCC Educational Foundation scholarship program

### ASPE — American Society of Plumbing Engineers
ASPE is the professional home for plumbing design engineers and engineering-minded master plumbers. It publishes the *Data Book*, a critical reference for system sizing and design.

### IAPMO — International Association of Plumbing and Mechanical Officials
IAPMO publishes the **Uniform Plumbing Code (UPC)** and the Uniform Mechanical Code (UMC), adopted by most western-states jurisdictions.

### ICC — International Code Council
The ICC publishes the **International Plumbing Code (IPC)** and the International Residential Code (IRC), adopted by most eastern and midwestern states.

## Lesson 1.4 — Why Plumbing Codes Exist: Public Health

Every plumbing installation is governed by adopted codes because improperly installed plumbing poses direct threats to public health:

- **Contaminated drinking water** — cross-connections between potable and non-potable systems can allow sewage, chemicals, or pathogens to enter the water supply.
- **Sewer gas (methane + hydrogen sulfide)** — without properly maintained traps and vents, toxic and flammable gases can migrate from the sewer into occupied buildings.
- **Legionella** — stagnant warm water in under-designed water-heater systems can harbor Legionella bacteria, the cause of Legionnaires' disease.
- **Scalding** — improperly set water-heater temperatures or missing tempering valves can cause severe burns, especially in children and the elderly.
- **Mold and structural damage** — leaking supply or drain lines cause moisture accumulation leading to mold growth and wood rot.

As a plumbing professional, your work is a first line of defense for the health and safety of building occupants.

## Lesson 1.5 — Job Outlook and Wages

According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS):

- **Employment growth:** Plumbers, pipefitters, and steamfitters are projected to grow approximately **6 %** over a ten-year period — faster than average for all occupations.
- **Median annual wage:** Approximately **$61,000–$65,000** nationwide (varies significantly by region, union affiliation, and specialty).
- **Top earners** (master plumbers in high-cost metro areas): > $100,000 per year.

**Drivers of demand:**
- Aging water infrastructure requiring replacement
- New residential and commercial construction
- Retrofitting buildings for water efficiency and green certification (LEED, WaterSense)
- Industrial and manufacturing piping (petrochemical, pharmaceutical)

## Module 1 Summary

- Plumbing has ancient roots; modern practice evolved from the Sanitary Revolution of the 1800s.
- The trade is structured in three license levels: **apprentice → journeyman → master**.
- Major organizations include the **UA**, **PHCC**, **ASPE**, **IAPMO**, and **ICC**.
- Codes protect public health by preventing contamination, sewer-gas intrusion, scalding, and water damage.
- Job outlook is strong; wages grow with experience and licensure.
